Addition and removal for the CS-121 reading list

I will argue that McCarthy's "Recursive Functions of Symbolic Expressions and Their Computation by Machine, Part I" should be added to the list, and, with some reluctance, that Nauer's "Report on the Algorithmic Language ALGOL 60" could be removed. At a high level, comparisons across bibliographic databases suggest that LISP has on on-going relevance that ALGOL does not. The case becomes stronger when we look at a few key ideas that McCarthy introduced, and latter papers that make particular reference to McCarthy's 1960 work.

Bibliometrics

Checking a citation index like Web of Science seems like a logical first step, and although the results are striking, I don't think they are a good representation of relative importance of these papers.

Since the 1980s, (McCarthy 1960) has averaged at or above 100 citations per year, while for (Backus 1959) there are scattered references, and none for (Nauer 1963). On the other hand, simply searching for "FORTRAN", "LISP", and "ALGOL" in article titles might give us a totally different idea of which ideas have remained influential. (I limited the categories searched to CS because there were a number of astronomy papers about the start Algol, and I didn't want that to skew the results.)

Other databases can give us an idea of how these terms have shown up outside the journal literature. WorldCat began as a shared cataloging platform for libraries, and more recently has added an article index.

The WorldCat article title search results are roughly similar to Web of Science, though there is an earlier rise for "FORTRAN". I am not sure why this is: WorldCat's depth of coverage during certain intervals may simply be different, or the category filter in Web of Science might make the difference. On the other hand, the results for books are clear, and markedly different than those for articles. This suggests that while there was some early interest in ALGOL, and in the 1980s and 1990s a spike in interest in LISP, FORTRAN has remained a foundational technology: Through the 1990s entire books focused on it, and while that has fallen off, it remains a focus in individual papers.

WorldCat also offers a language filter which might give us a sense of how interest in different technologies has varied geographically. All media are taken together here because of the smaller result counts. (These results should be viewed with caution: Even a cursory look at the details will reveal a number of items which seem to be incorrectly characterized. My sense is that if one paper in a larger collection is in a non-English language, or if the volume is published by a European organization, it might come up in the filtered results.) That said, we begin to get a sense that ALGOL was signficantly more important in continental Europe than elsewhere.

Widening the lens further, we can look at how "FORTRAN", "ALGOL", and "LISP" are used across all the printed material which Google has indexed.

The French, German, and Italian corpora all show an early period of frequent use of the word "ALGOL": Until the later 1960s, it is more frequent than "FORTRAN". On the other hand, in American English "ALGOL" was always negligible. The trends in Spanish, likely dominated by Latin America, mirror American English, though the frequencies are an order of magnitude lower. British English is perhaps more similar to Continental Europe than it is to North America.

This has been digressive but I think the important parts are:

  • Wider searches on corpora can show trends that aren't obvious from limited article indexes.
  • Computer Science is geographically heterogeneous: Viewed from Europe, the work on ALGOL might be a classic, even if that's not the case here.
  • The recognition of a classic may take some time. What changed in the 1980s to give McCarthy's article, and the LISP language, an importance it hadn't seen in the preceding two decades?
